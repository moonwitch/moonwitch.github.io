For a long time, the word "accommodation" felt heavy to me. It sounded like a special favour, a medical necessity that I had to prove with a pile of paperwork and a defensive posture. In the corporate world, we’re taught to be interchangeable parts in a machine, and asking for a different "setting" feels like admitting you're a broken gear.

I’ve stopped looking at it that way. Now, I think of it as my **Accommodations Stack**. Just like we choose a specific IDE, a particular cloud provider, or a set of monitoring tools to make our code run better, we can choose the tools and environment settings that make our *brains* run better.

Here is how I’ve built my stack, and how you can start building yours — without the "HR fear."

## 1. Identify the Friction, Not the Diagnosis

When you're starting this process, you don't actually have to lead with your diagnosis. In fact, it's often more effective to lead with the **technical requirement**.

Instead of saying "I have ADHD and I'm struggling," try:
- "I find that I'm significantly more productive when I can focus on deep work without auditory interruptions. I’d like to use noise-canceling headphones as a standard part of my kit."
- "My processing style works best with written instructions. Can we ensure that all task assignments come with a brief summary in the ticket or via email?"

## 2. The Low-Stakes Entry Points

You don't always need a formal meeting to change your stack. Some of the most powerful accommodations are "stealth" ones:
- **Calendar Blocking:** I block out "Focus Time" and "Administrative Recovery." It looks like a meeting, so people respect the boundary, but it's actually my executive-function buffer.
- **Async-First Communication:** I explicitly tell my team, "If it's not an emergency, please Slack me instead of tapping me on the shoulder. It helps me stay in the zone."

## 3. Tooling Your Environment

As a DevOps engineer, I love a good tool. My physical and digital stack includes:
- **Loop Earplugs:** (Yes, I work there, but I used them long before I did.) They help me stay present in the office without the sensory "static" of an open plan.
- **The 'Second Read' AI:** As I’ve written about before, using AI to decode tone or draft measured responses is a genuine communication scaffold.
- **Multiple Monitors for 'Context Mapping':** One screen for the task, one for the documentation, one for the 'big picture' monitoring. It reduces the cognitive load of tab-switching.

## 4. Having the "Formal" Conversation

If you do need to involve management or HR, keep it focused on **outcomes**. 

"I want to ensure I'm delivering at the high standard I set for myself. To do that reliably, I’ve found that I need [X]. This allows me to bypass [Friction Y] and get straight to the work."

When you frame it as a performance optimization rather than a request for help, the dynamic changes. You aren't asking for a "pass"; you're asking for the right hardware to run your software.

## The Takeaway

Your brain is the most expensive piece of equipment your company has. It is in *everyone's* best interest that it runs at its best. Building your Accommodations Stack isn't about being "difficult" — it's about being an engineer who knows how to maintain their most critical system.

What's the first tool in your stack going to be?
